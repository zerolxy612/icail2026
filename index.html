ICAIL 2026

Trustworthy Legal Reasoning :
From Generative AI to Verified Legal Intelligence

June 12, 2026, Singapore Management University, Singapore

About TLR 2026

As Large Language Models (LLMs) increasingly assist with high-stakes legal tasks, ranging from contract drafting and case research to judicial analytics, a central question has emerged: Can generative systems deliver verifiable, up-to-date, and jurisdiction-aware answers with auditable reasoning steps?

This workshop, Trustworthy Legal Reasoning (TLR 2026), addresses this question through the lens of Verified Generation for Legal AI. Based on the comprehensive survey by Han et al., this workshop aims to define a roadmap for "Governance-Native" systems. We propose a methodological shift from merely maximizing preference alignment to ensuring verifiable correctness via robust evidence grounding, retrieval of authoritative legal sources, and logic-based checks.

Specific objectives include:

  • Unifying Frameworks: Exploring architectures that pair pre-trained LLMs with verifiability modules to cross-check claims against statutes and case law.
  • Epistemic Reliability: Discussing validation methods via paradigms of refutation and conflict, enabling systems to recognize their own competence limits.
  • Benchmarking & Evaluation: Addressing the lack of standard metrics for factual reliability in law and developing automated evaluation methods for jurisdictional limits and doctrinal variations.

Research Themes

Topics of Interest

1. Trustworthy Legal Reasoning

  • Reasoning quality in legal question answering and decision support
  • Fact-sensitive and norm-sensitive legal analysis
  • Structured reasoning from issue identification to conclusion
  • Legal argumentation that is transparent, reviewable, and contestable
  • Bridging generative outputs and legally reliable reasoning

2. From Generative AI to Verified Legal Intelligence

  • Retrieval-grounded and citation-supported legal generation
  • Verified legal intelligence beyond fluent text generation
  • Source-traceable reasoning and authority-aware outputs
  • Integration of statutes, cases and regulations
  • Systems that distinguish plausible language from valid legal justification

3. Human-AI Collaboration in Legal Reasoning

  • Lawyer-AI and judge-AI interaction in high-stakes contexts
  • Interfaces for review, verification, and correction
  • Collaborative workflows for drafting, analysis, and dispute handling
  • Human oversight, escalation, and responsibility allocation
  • Designing systems that support judgment rather than replace it

4. Explainability, Fairness & Accountability

  • Explainable legal reasoning and justification disclosure
  • Bias detection and mitigation in legal AI systems
  • Procedural fairness and informed participation
  • Auditability, contestability, and error correction
  • Accountability across developers, deployers, and institutional users

5. Evaluation, Benchmarks & Legal Data Infrastructure

  • Benchmarks for legal reasoning, faithfulness, and reliability
  • Empirical evaluation of legal AI systems
  • Datasets for traceable, verifiable, and jurisdiction-sensitive tasks
  • Structured legal data for retrieval, reasoning, and validation
  • Metrics for hallucination, legal accuracy, and reasoning quality

6. Governance and Verified Deployment

  • Compliance with related legal frameworks
  • Risk governance for legal and justice-oriented AI
  • Deployment standards for trustworthy legal systems
  • Evidence, provenance, and institutional assurance mechanisms
  • Responsible pathways from prototype to public-facing legal tools
